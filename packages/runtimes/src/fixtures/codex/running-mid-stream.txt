• Distributed consensus is the problem of getting a group of machines to agree
  on one value or one ordered sequence of values even when some machines crash,
  messages are delayed, messages arrive out of order, or parts of the network
  temporarily cannot communicate. The hard part is not agreement when everything
  works; it is preserving safety when everything is ambiguous. A server may not
  know whether another server is slow, disconnected, or dead, and two groups of
  servers may each believe they should make progress. Consensus protocols solve
  this by requiring decisions to be supported by overlapping majorities, or
  quorums. In a cluster of five, any majority has at least three nodes, and any
  two majorities must share at least one node. That overlap is crucial: it
  prevents two conflicting values from both being chosen, because at least one
  participant would have knowledge of the earlier decision or promise that
  constrains later decisions. Most consensus systems assume crash faults, not
  Byzantine faults, meaning nodes may stop or restart but do not lie
  maliciously.
