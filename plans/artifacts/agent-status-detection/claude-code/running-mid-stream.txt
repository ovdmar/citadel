❯ Think deeply about how to design a distributed cache invalidation system.
  Take your time, consider multiple angles, write at least 5 paragraphs.

● Distributed cache invalidation is fundamentally a consistency problem
  disguised as a performance problem. The cache exists because the source of
  truth is too slow or too expensive to hit on every read, but the moment you
  duplicate state across nodes you inherit every hard problem in distributed
  systems: ordering, partial failure, clock skew, and the impossibility of
  simultaneous updates. Before designing anything, the first question to answer
  is what consistency model the application actually needs. Strong consistency
  (a write is never followed by a stale read anywhere) is expensive and often
  unnecessary; bounded staleness (reads may lag by at most N seconds) is
  dramatically cheaper and usually sufficient; eventual consistency is cheapest
  but exposes the application to surprising read-your-writes violations. The
  design space collapses dramatically once this is pinned down, and skipping
  this step is the most common reason cache systems end up with elaborate
  machinery that solves the wrong problem.
